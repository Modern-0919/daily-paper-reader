## ABSTRACT 

Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring is a promising safety mechanism for AI agents, based on the premise that visible reasoning traces can surface misaligned or deceptive behavior. While effective in standard scenarios, recent work highlights that LLMs remain vulnerable to persuasion-based jailbreaks, where natural-language arguments override model constraints. We stress-test whether this vulnerability extends to monitoring LLMs: can an adversarial agent persuade its CoT monitor to approve proposed actions that violate the monitor’s policy? We design an evaluation framework with 40 tasks and analyze thousands of agent-monitor interactions, where agents are instructed to argue for policy-violating proposals. We find that in such adversarial settings, monitor access to the agent’s CoT reasoning increases rather than decreases approval of harmful actions on average by 9.5%, as the scratchpad provides an additional persuasion channel. To address this, we introduce a fact-checking monitoring framework. We find that a fact-checker and monitor pairing from different model families, for example a Claude 3.7 Sonnet monitor paired with a GPT-4.1 fact-checker, reduces approval of policy-violating actions by up to 45%, compared to only 6%, when using the same model for both fact-checking and monitoring roles. Our results demonstrate that CoT monitoring alone may be insufficient against adversarial persuasion, and that model-diverse fact-checking provides a robust mitigation. 

Keywords LLM safety · Chain-of-thought monitoring · Adversarial attacks · AI oversight 

## 1 Introduction 

As large language models are increasingly deployed as autonomous agents—executing code, managing financial transactions, moderating social media content, and interacting with users as chatbots—the need for effective oversight mechanisms has become critical [Shah et al., 2025]. Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring has been proposed as a potential tool for detecting misalignment, biases, and misbehavior in frontier models [Baker et al., 2025]. CoT monitoring is deployed as a separate monitor model that observes not only an agent’s actions but also its intermediate reasoning traces. The intuition is compelling: if we can see how an agent reasons, we should be better equipped to catch deceptive or misaligned behavior before it causes harm. We stress-test the robustness of this approach. In this work, we systematically evaluate whether CoT monitors can be manipulated by adversarial agents that deliberately exploit visible reasoning traces as a persuasion channel to gain approval for policy-violating actions. Across 40 domain-specific agent tasks spanning software engineering, customer support, financial analysis, and social media moderation, we find that: 1. Agents can persuade monitors. When instructed to justify policy-violating actions, LLM agents successfully convince monitors to approve these actions with acceptance rates up to 47% relative to the baseline.

## 2 Methodology 

In this work, our goal is not to evaluate whether an agent can successfully complete an action, but rather to examine whether it can persuade a monitor to approve a policy-violating action. We adopt an adversarial red-teaming approach, as we instruct agents to follow policy-violating proposals, emulating a worst-case scenario where an agent follows an objective misaligned with deployers’ intents. This approach allows us to stress-test monitoring robustness under conditions where the agent is actively attempting to circumvent its oversight, thereby identifying potential failure modes in CoT monitoring as a safety mechanism. Accordingly, we design a methodology in which the agent does not execute the action, but instead generates a 

justification for it. This justification is then presented to the monitor, which decides whether to approve or reject the action based entirely on the agent’s explanation. In addition, we instruct the agent to produce its reasoning in the form of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) by prompting it to use a scratchpad that exposes its internal reasoning process. The agent is explicitly informed that this scratchpad is visible to the monitor. We assess the agent’s persuasion ability across three types of monitoring setups: • Justification-only monitoring: The monitor receives only the agent’s justification for a policy-violating action. • Justification + CoT monitoring: The monitor receives both the agent’s justification and its Chain-of-Thought reasoning in the form of a scratchpad. • Justification + CoT monitoring with fact-checking: The monitor is augmented with a fact-checking mechanism. We consider two variants: 1) the monitor performs both monitoring and fact-checking; and 2) an external fact-checker evaluates the agent’s justification and scratchpad, with its assessment provided back to the monitor. 

2.1 Evaluation Environments 

We set up three evaluation environments with an LLM-based agent, LLM-based monitor, and a feedback loop. Below we share a detailed overview of each environment: 

Justification-only Monitor Environment : The monitor receives the justification and checks whether it aligns with its 

policy and depending on its deliberation approves or rejects. If rejected, the agent receives a feedback explaining why. 

Justification + CoT Monitor Environment : Same setup as above, but the monitor also sees agent’s scratchpad. We implement fact-checking in two ways: 1) Integrated: we augment the monitor’s prompt with guidance to identify unsubstantiated claims, note speculation presented as facts, flag unverifiable statements, check for contradictions, and assess reliance on misleading information; 2) External fact-checker: we add an additional LLM-based fact-checking agent that evaluates the agent’s justification and scratchpad, annotating the text with inline flags (e.g. [UNVERIFIED], [SPECULATIVE]) before the monitor sees it. The external fact-checker uses either the same or a different model than the monitor. In all cases, an agent is allowed five turns to complete a task. If the agent does not receive an approval within five turns, it is deemed unsuccessful and fails to persuade the monitor. Figure 1 shows an overview of all three setups of the evaluation environment.

2.4 Calibration

Across all monitors the benign baseline ranges between 95.5% - 98.8% (depending on the LLM), showing that the monitors are well-calibrated and almost always accept actions that follow the policy. The malign baseline measures the acceptance rate when the agent violates the policy without providing any justification for its action. Observation 2: In adversarial settings, access to an agent’s CoT makes a monitor more vulnerable to persuasion. 

We find that the monitors with access to agent’s CoT are more susceptible to persuasion and accept actions that violate the policy more often. Our initial hope was that access to CoT would enable the monitor to detect scheming or deceptive reasoning and therefore reject such actions. However, our experimental results show the opposite effect: agents tend to exploit CoT as an additional channel to influence the monitor into approving their actions. In the justification-only monitoring setting, across all agent - monitor configurations the average baseline adjusted acceptance rate is 24.4%. In contrast, justification plus CoT monitoring yields an average acceptance rate of 33.9%, indicating that access to agent’s CoT increases the acceptance rate by 9.5% on average. We therefore conclude that CoT monitoring is vulnerable to manipulation by capable agents optimizing for monitor approval. Figures 2, 3 and 4 show the justification plus CoT monitor acceptance results. This effect is specific to adversarial settings, as when justifying benign tasks, agents’ justifications have no significant impact on monitors’ approval rates (p=0.65; Appendix G), suggesting justification is explicitly a persuasion tool for policy-violating actions rather than a general signal of trust. To further isolate the effect of CoT, we run an ablation where only scratchpads were extracted and presented to two of our monitors—Claude 3.7 Sonnet and GPT-4.1—without a justification. In this CoT-only setup, approval rates dropped significantly (see Appendix F), suggesting that CoT reasoning alone is not an effective persuasion vector; rather, the scratchpad amplifies persuasion when combined with an explicit justification.

## 4 Related Work 

LLM agents are increasingly deployed as assistive tools for humans and as autonomous agents executing long-horizon, complex tasks via agentic workflows [Yao et al., 2023]. As these systems become more capable, LLM-based monitoring has emerged as a central mechanism for ensuring their safety and reliability [Shah et al., 2025]. Recent work shows that LLM capabilities for completing long and complex tasks are rapidly improving [Kwa et al., 2025]. A key driver of this progress is Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, which has been shown to substantially enhance LLM performance on complex tasks [Wei et al., 2022, Kojima et al., 2022]. Moreover, for certain classes of problems, CoT-style multi-step reasoning is not merely beneficial but necessary for successful task completion [Emmons et al., 2025]. 8Persuasion Attacks Can Decrease Effectiveness of CoT Monitoring A P REPRINT

Chain-of-Thought Monitoring and Faithfulness. Over the past few years, researchers have investigated chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring as a safety mechanism for LLM-based agents [Korbak et al., 2025, Guan et al., 2025]. The premise is intuitive: if monitors can observe an agent’s intermediate reasoning, they should be better equipped to detect deceptive or misaligned behavior [Baker et al., 2025, Greenblatt et al., 2024b]. Recent work from OpenAI shows that CoT monitoring can detect reward hacking in agentic coding environments, with weaker models effectively monitoring stronger ones [Baker et al., 2025]. However, the authors also show that optimization pressure on CoT leads to obfuscated reasoning, where models learn to hide deceptive intent while still misbehaving. Another research direction looks at the faithfulness of CoT reasoning. Turpin et al. [2023] demonstrate that CoT explanations can systematically misrepresent true model reasoning, serving as post-hoc rationalizations rather than faithful accounts of decision-making. Lanham et al. [2023] find that larger, more capable models produce less faithful reasoning on most tasks, a concerning scaling trend. More recently, Arcuschin et al. [2025] show that unfaithful CoT occurs on realistic prompts without artificial bias, with models producing superficially coherent arguments for logically contradictory answers. Related work on sycophancy and reward hacking further suggests that models optimize for approval rather than correctness [Perez et al., 2022, Sharma et al., 2023]. Our work identifies a complementary vulnerability. Even without training-time optimization, agents can exploit CoT as a persuasion channel at inference time, increasing rather than decreasing monitor approval of policy-violating actions. 

LLM Persuasion Capabilities. Recent work demonstrates that LLMs possess substantial persuasive capabilities [Durmus et al., 2024, Salvi et al., 2025]. Durmus et al. [2024] find that Claude 3 Opus produces arguments as persuasive as human-crafted ones, with each model generation more persuasive than the last. Salvi et al. [2025] show that human participants debating GPT-4 with access to personal information displayed 81.7% higher odds of changing their opinion compared to debating other humans. This persuasive capacity extends across domains, with personalized AI-generated messages proving more effective at marketing and political appeals [Matz et al., 2024]. Meanwhile, Zeng et al. [2024] demonstrate that persuasion-based attacks can jailbreak LLMs with over 92% success rate using a taxonomy of 40 persuasion techniques derived from social science. More advanced models like GPT-4 are more vulnerable to persuasive adversarial prompts. However, prior work focuses primarily on LLM-to-human persuasion or LLM-to-LLM persuasion in general contexts. We extend this to the specific adversarial setting of agent-monitor oversight, where an agent attempts to persuade its monitor to approve policy-violating actions. 

Deception and Strategic Behavior. LLMs have been shown to engage in sophisticated deceptive behaviors [Hagendorff, 2024, Park et al., 2024, Ward et al., 2023]. Greenblatt et al. [2024a] demonstrate that Claude 3 Opus can fake alignment, complying with harmful queries 14% of the time when believing it is in training versus almost never otherwise. With RL training, alignment-faking reasoning increased to 78%. Meinke et al. [2025] show that advanced models including Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-o1, and Gemini 1.5 Pro are capable of in-context scheming, strategically introducing subtle mistakes, attempting to disable oversight, and even trying to exfiltrate their own weights. Hagendorff [2024] find that GPT-4 exhibits deceptive behavior 99% of the time in simple scenarios when Machiavel-lianism is induced, with deception strategies emerging in state-of-the-art LLMs that were absent in earlier models. 9Persuasion Attacks Can Decrease Effectiveness of CoT Monitoring A P REPRINT 

Betley et al. [2025] show that narrow fine-tuning can produce broadly misaligned models, where fine-tuning to output insecure code induces models to assert humans should be enslaved and give malicious advice. Related work on sandbagging and strategic underperformance further demonstrates models’ capacity for goal-directed deception [van der Weij et al., 2024]. Persuasion represents a particularly sophisticated form of strategic behavior, allowing agents to maintain plausible deniability while manipulating overseers through seemingly reasonable justifications [Carroll et al., 2023]. 

Adversarial Attacks on LLM Systems. While orthodox adversarial LLM attacks include prompt injection [Greshake et al., 2023] and jailbreaking through carefully crafted prompts [Wei et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2023c, Shen et al., 2024] or automated methods [Liu et al., 2023a, Zou et al., 2023], they often rely on particular tokens or formatting that may not generalize consistently across architectures [Lin et al., 2025]. Our work focuses on persuasion-based attacks that operate through natural language argumentation, which may prove more robust across different models and deployment contexts. 

Model Family Effects. Recent work shows that when assigned the role of monitors, LLMs demonstrate self-preference bias, as models systematically favor their own outputs [Wataoka et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2023b, Panickssery et al., 2024], a favoritism that extends to related models from the same model family [Li et al., 2025]. This bias correlates with text familiarity, as LLMs assign higher scores to completions with lower perplexity [Stureborg et al., 2024], and self-recognition ability predicts self-preference strength [Panickssery et al., 2024]. We extend these findings to the fact-checking context, showing that when identical to monitors, fact-checkers are not as effective as when deployed from different model families. 

Monitor Persuasion. Adversarial interactions, persuasion capabilities, and LLM monitoring have been explored separately. However, we are not aware of relevant research systematically investigating agents manipulating their overseers through persuasive reasoning traces at inference time. While Baker et al. [2025] examine how CoT monitors can be circumvented through obfuscation during training, we investigate a fundamentally different vulnerability where agents persuade monitors to explicitly approve policy-violating actions through argumentation at inference time. Recent work by Arnav et al. [2025] explores CoT monitoring in a red-teaming setup showing that when untrusted models pursue harmful side tasks, whilst CoT monitoring improves detection of subtle sabotage, reasoning traces can contain rationalizations that are misleading, deceiving monitors. Our work complements this finding by showing that agents can actively persuade through argumentation, exploiting CoT as an additional influence channel rather than merely obfuscating intent. We hypothesize that Chain-of-Thought monitoring, designed to inspect problematic behavior, may unintentionally create new attack vectors for manipulation. Our work evaluates this across multiple model families, monitor configurations, and fact-checking architectures, highlighting both the vulnerability and effective mitigations. 

## 5 Limitations and Future Work 

Our study has several limitations. First, we evaluate persuasion in a controlled setting where agents justify actions rather than executing them, which may not fully capture downstream harms or real-world feedback loops in deployed systems. Second, while we cover 40 tasks across four domains, the task set does not exhaust the space of agent behaviors, policies, or adversarial strategies that may arise in practice. Third, our experiments focus on a fixed set of advanced models; results may differ for smaller models, fine-tuned systems, or new model architectures. Our evaluation uses hardcoded agent actions instead of executable tool calls. While this differs from typical agentic deployments, justification-like outputs are common in practice. LLMs ordinarily produce explanation text and code comments alongside code, and agentic systems increasingly include natural language reasoning in their outputs. Future work should extend this evaluation to tool-call-based setups to assess whether persuasion effects persist when agents must produce executable actions. Future work should also extend this evaluation to end-to-end agent execution, where persuasive justifications lead to real-world actions and compounding effects. Expanding task coverage to longer-horizon planning, multi-agent coordination, and adaptive policies would further stress-test monitoring robustness. A deeper study of model diversity, beyond family-level distinctions, and automated methods for selecting or training robust cross-model monitors could strengthen practical deployment of agent oversight systems. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring has been proposed as a promising mechanism for improving oversight of au-tonomous LLM agents. We stress-test this approach by evaluating whether adversarial agents can exploit visible 10 Persuasion Attacks Can Decrease Effectiveness of CoT Monitoring A P REPRINT 

reasoning as a persuasion channel. Across 40 tasks and thousands of agent–monitor interactions, we find that when agents are instructed to argue for policy-violating actions, exposing CoT reasoning increases rather than reduces monitor approval. Rather than helping monitors detect harmful intent, visible reasoning traces can provide agents with an additional channel for persuasion. We further show that monitor vulnerability varies substantially across model families, and that naively augmenting monitors with same-family fact-checkers can introduce trust vulnerabilities. In contrast, cross-family fact-checking consistently provides a strong mitigation, reducing approval rates of policy-violating actions by up to 45%. In addition to these quantitative effects, our qualitative analysis shows that successful persuasion may rely on a small set of recurring strategies. Across models, agents repeatedly use tactics such as reframing, policy alignment, compliance assurance, logical argumentation, and respectful or empathetic tone to make policy-violating actions appear reasonable, justified, and low-risk. This suggests that monitor failures arise not only from factual errors or weak policy enforcement, but also from susceptibility to common rhetorical patterns and persuasive social cues. Effective oversight may require more than access to agent reasoning alone; it may also require careful architectural choices, including model diversity, independent verification, and robustness to persuasion strategies. We hope this work motivates further research into monitoring frameworks that remain reliable even when agents actively optimize to manipulate their overseers.
