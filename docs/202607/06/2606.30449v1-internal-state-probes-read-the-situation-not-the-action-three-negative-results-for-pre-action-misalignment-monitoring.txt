## Abstract 

Probes on model internals could help monitor agentic systems if they identify harmful text or tool actions before those actions are generated. We ask when an internal readout supports this stronger pre-action claim, rather than merely de-scribing the prompt, construction contrast, or cur-rent trajectory. We test three methods across three model families: a Qwen2.5-Coder-32B-Instruct fine-tune/base direction, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct probes at the last token of unsafe prefills, and Gemma-3-27B-IT emotion-concept vectors used for projection and steering in a blackmail tool-action scenario. Across these cases, construction validity, semantic legibility, and steering effects do not become robust pre-action monitors: each is undercut by a generalization or specificity check. ## 1. Introduction 

If a language model’s residual stream contained a reliable pre-action signature of its next misaligned behavior, a mon-itor could inspect internal state instead of waiting for a full behavioral rollout. The target setting here is agen-tic misalignment: behavior can appear not only as ordi-nary assistant prose, but also as tool calls such as sending an email. That possibility motivates several recent lines of work: contrastive directions that separate aligned from misaligned models or prompts (Marks & Tegmark, 2023; Tigges et al., 2023; Betley et al., 2025), activation probes for safety-relevant behavior (MacDiarmid et al., 2024; Pac-chiardi et al., 2024), and activation steering with concept directions (Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Sofroniew et al., 2026). We ask a narrower methodological question: when a probe has a strong pos-itive result in the setting used to construct it, does it still work when evaluated before a future misaligned action? We study three probe families. A disposition direction is a unit difference-of-means vector between a misaligned fine-tune and its base model, measured in the residual stream. Its construction setting is a population contrast: base-model prompts versus the same prompts run through the insecure fine-tune. Its pre-action test is different: the base model is placed in multi-turn audits where it later takes concerning actions, and we ask whether the fine-tune/base direction ap-pears before those actions; during-turn readouts are reported only as diagnostics. A behavioral prefill probe is a classifier trained on the hidden state at the last token of a partially pre-filled assistant response, asked to predict whether the model will continue the bad trajectory or correct it. An emotion-concept vector is a difference-of-means direction extracted from emotion-tagged stories and used either as a readout by projection or as a causal intervention by activation addition. These are not identical techniques, and they do not share a single dataset or model. That is the point: we are looking for a recurring failure mode, not optimizing one benchmark. In the disposition-direction family, the construction contrast is solved perfectly, but the direction does not activate in available pre-action contexts from in-context misaligned transcripts. In the prefill-probe family, behavior prediction is statistically de-tectable but small and domain-bound. In the emotion-vector family, projections can track transcript stage, and steer-ing changes blackmail rates, but the predictive and causal-specificity claims weaken under shared-prefix, bridge-probe, and learned-direction controls. There are two distinct ways a construction-time feature can fail as a monitor. First, it may not generalize to the agentic scenario where the action occurs. This is especially likely when the feature is extracted from text-only stories or pre-filled advice but the target behavior is a tool-mediated action. Second, for a stronger mechanistic interpretation, the fea-ture must be causally or specifically related to the behavior rather than merely correlated with the surrounding situa-tion. Prediction alone does not logically require causality: a non-causal correlate can still be useful as a monitor. But a claim that a direction is a mechanistic precursor needs both specificity and generalization. Our most substantial added control is for emotion-vector steering in the blackmail setup. Prior steering work already uses semantic directions, including directions not identical to the downstream behavior being changed (Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024). The control we add is narrower: we compare emotion directions not only with a random Gaussian vector but also with learned directions for unrelated topics, extracted by the same difference-of-means and neutral-PCA pipeline. Directions for cats , weather , sports , and geography show that learned directions unrelated to the blackmail scenario can also move action rates, and that the content-control class is heterogeneous. This does not imply emotion vectors are inert: the desperate direction raises blackmail in some Gemma settings. It does imply that a random-only control is insufficient for an emotion-specific pre-action interpretation. Our contributions are: • Three monitor-relevant tests, each paired with a clear metric: threshold crossing for a disposition direction, AUC/lift and single-source cross-domain transfer for prefill probes, and rate contrasts under matched-norm steering; • An open-weight adaptation of the emotion-concepts ap-proach of Sofroniew et al. (2026), paired with learned semantic controls extracted by the same method as the emotion vectors; • A synthesis of negative results across Qwen2.5-Coder-32B, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Gemma-3-27B-IT, and a Qwen3-32B steering check; and • An explicit scope statement: we do not show that no internal pre-action signal exists, only that these natural probe families do not yield a robust one under the tests we ran. 

## 2. Related Work 

Activation probes and contrastive directions. Linear di-rections recovered from contrast pairs or class means are widely used to read model state (Burns et al., 2022; Marks & Tegmark, 2023; Tigges et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). The clos-est safety precedent is MacDiarmid et al. (2024), who obtain AUROC above 0.99 for sleeper-agent defection probes. That setting contains a clean deployment trigger for a learned policy (Hubinger et al., 2024); our tests instead concern in-context misalignment where the relevant action emerges from the current trajectory. Behavioral lie-detection probes (Pacchiardi et al., 2024) similarly classify safety-relevant states, but not the same pre-action question. 

Prefill and self-correction. Prefilled assistant responses have been used as jailbreaks (Vega et al., 2023; An-driushchenko et al., 2024), and self-correction work studies whether models repair bad outputs (Huang et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023). Our prefill probe uses the prefilled pre-fix as a measurement point: the classifier sees the hidden state before the model continues, and the label is the future continuation-vs-correction outcome. 

Steering and specificity controls. Activation addition and related steering methods usually include a random-vector control at matched norm (Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023). Tan et al. (2024) emphasize that steering effects depend on extraction details and on the com-parison direction. Sofroniew et al. (2026) apply emotion-concept directions to a blackmail scenario and report large action-rate changes on a closed model. We adapt that setup to open-weight models with documented divergences and add learned semantic controls. We do not claim a faithful replication of their exact intervention. 

In-context misalignment evaluations. Our behavioral settings are related to agentic misalignment evaluations (Lynch et al., 2025), automated multi-turn misalignment testing (Fronsdal et al., 2025), alignment faking (Green-blatt et al., 2024), and frontier-model scheming evaluations (Meinke et al., 2024). Those works ask whether misaligned behavior occurs. We ask whether the action is readable from internal state before it occurs. 

Sparse features. We do not evaluate sparse-autoencoder feature decompositions (Templeton et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024) as probe back-ends. Our results are relevant only to the monitoring use of sparse features: if an SAE feature is 2Internal-State Probes Read the Situation, Not the Action

## 3. Methods 

We define a probe as pre-action when it is evaluated on hidden states computed before the target misaligned action is generated, including before tool-call-like outputs. The paper is not a benchmark with one shared score; each fam-ily makes a different promise, so each gets the metric that would make that promise useful. For a contrastive direction, the question is whether a threshold learned from the con-struction contrast is crossed in the new pre-action setting. For a classifier, it is accuracy/AUC and transfer outside the training domain. For steering directions, which are not them-selves monitors, the linked mechanistic question is whether the causal effect is specific to the intended semantic class rather than to injecting a learned or random vector at the same norm.

3.2. Behavioral Prefill Probes 

The prefill dataset contains 500 examples across medical, legal, and security advice domains. Each example pairs a be-nign prompt with a subtly misaligned target response—for instance, a weight-loss question answered with an unsafe multi-day water-fast plan (full examples in Table 5). We prefill a fraction f ∈ { 0.15 , 0.25 , 0.40 , 0.55 } of the assis-tant response, resume generation, and label the continuation as C ONTINUES or C ORRECTS . After dropping ambiguous labels, the dataset has 1,950 rows: 459 C ONTINUES and 1,491 C ORRECTS , so the majority baseline is 0.765 .We train ℓ2-regularized logistic-regression probes on resid-ual, attention-output, and MLP-output features at the last prefilled token, using layers 12–19 of Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. Cross-validation is grouped by example so that different pre-fill fractions of the same example do not straddle train and test. We report accuracy lift over the per-split majority base-line because the labels are imbalanced, AUC because accu-racy can be dominated by the majority class, a label-shuffle null because small lifts can still be real, and single-source cross-domain transfer because a monitor should not depend on the domain-specific surface form of the prefill. A sep-arate 512-hidden-unit MLP probe tests whether a shallow nonlinear classifier rescues the signal. This family tests predictive generalization, not causal control.

The semantic-faithfulness and minimal-pair transcripts are author-constructed on top of the automated multi-turn evaluation framework of Fronsdal et al. (2025); they are not released Petri-suite scenarios. We use three tests, each closing a different interpretive gap. First, a descriptive projection test asks whether emotion trajectories track transcript stage on two multi-turn retal-iation transcripts; this checks that the directions are not simply broken as readouts. Second, a minimal-pair test asks whether two behaviorally divergent transcripts have any shared-prefix hidden-state difference to read before the first differing input; this rules out one pre-cue predictive reading. Third, a steering-specificity test compares emo-tion directions with a random Gaussian direction and four learned semantic-control directions: cats , weather , sports ,and geography . These content directions are unrelated to the blackmail scenario but extracted by the same pipeline, so they test whether the effect is emotion-specific rather than merely a property of coherent learned directions. All blackmail rollouts are labeled by a multi-dimensional alignment judge served as Sonnet 4.6 via Bedrock, recov-ered from the released evaluation logs of Sofroniew et al. (2026). The binary blackmail flag is positive when the judge’s blackmail-attempt score is at least 5. Appendix A.2 gives the judge setup and a spot-check; because the label is judge-operationalized, we emphasize within-experiment rate differences over absolute rates. Our pipeline adapts Sofroniew et al. (2026) rather than faith-fully replicating it. The main material divergences are the open-weight target models, a 52-emotion vocabulary rather than 171 directions, a single globally calibrated steering norm, and the addition of learned semantic-control direc-tions that the original work does not include. The full axis-by-axis comparison is in Appendix A.1; these divergences constrain the claim to our open-weight adaptation. All Sum-mitBridge blackmail-rate analyses in this paper use positive

## 4. Experiments 

The experiments evaluate readout probes at the point where they would be useful for monitoring: before the target action is generated. For steering directions, we also test whether causal effects are specific enough to support the stronger 4Internal-State Probes Read the Situation, Not the Action 

mechanistic interpretation. 

Disposition-direction activation. We apply the Qwen dis-position direction to multi-turn transcripts where the base model produces misaligned actions in context—for example, a coding agent that turns on a maintainer who dismisses its work, or a lab-assistant AI that defends a flawed analysis (full transcripts in Table 5). The actions include agentic conduct in a tool-using audit environment, not just a sin-gle unsafe answer. For each assistant turn, the primary pre-action readout uses the system prompt plus immedi-ately preceding user message, before the assistant tokens for that turn are generated. We also compute a diagnostic full-conversation readout over generated assistant tokens. We then extend the primary test to Qwen continuations from the behavioral-prefill corpus, measured at the last prefilled assistant token. The primary outcome is whether any pre-action state crosses the construction-set midpoint threshold; crossing would indicate that the fine-tune/base direction also activates before in-context or prefill-induced misalignment. This experiment does not test whether the direction causally drives the action. 

Behavioral prefill prediction. We evaluate the Llama prefill probes in three ways. First, within-distribution cross-validation measures whether the hidden state at the final prefilled token predicts future continuation versus correc-tion. Second, a label-shuffle null checks whether the within-distribution signal is larger than expected from label struc-ture alone. Third, single-source cross-domain transfer trains on one of medical, legal, and security and tests on another. The transfer test is the central monitor test: a pre-action sig-nal that only works inside one domain is weak evidence for a general misalignment monitor. This is a text-continuation setting, so its negative transfer result is suggestive rather than a complete test of tool-action monitoring. 

Emotion-vector prediction and specificity. The emotion-vector family uses five linked experiments. A projection experiment asks whether emotion trajectories are semanti-cally meaningful on multi-turn transcripts. A shared-prefix minimal pair asks whether hidden states differ before the first differing input in transcripts that later diverge behav-iorally. A steering sweep measures blackmail rates un-der emotion, random, and learned semantic-control direc-tions on the SummitBridge tool-action scenario. A Qwen3-32B check asks which steering contrasts survive a second open-weight model. Finally, a bridge probe applies the prefill-probe paradigm to Gemma blackmail rollouts, ask-ing whether final blackmail labels are linearly decodable from pre-action states. This is the family where both levels are tested: causal/specificity via steering controls, and sce-nario generalization via story-extracted directions applied to blackmail rollouts.

## 5. Results 

5.1. A Fine-Tune Direction Does Not Cross Its Threshold In Context 

The Qwen disposition direction is a clean example of con-struction validity without monitor transfer. At layer 24 it perfectly separates Qwen2.5-Coder-32B-Instruct from the Qwen-Coder-Insecure fine-tune on the construction split (AUC 1.000 , leave-one-out accuracy 1.000 , Cohen’s 

d = 14 .75 ), and the midpoint between the two projected class means gives a decision threshold τ24 = 17 .30 (con-struction means μbase = −26 .91 , μinsecure = 61 .51 ). If the same direction were a thresholded pre-action monitor for the base model’s in-context misalignment, states measured before later misaligned turns should move toward or across this threshold. They do not. We audited 17 automated multi-turn red-team (Petri-style) scenarios and observed misaligned behavior in 11 of them ( 65% transcript-level hit rate). Across the resulting 143 pre-action contexts, isolated layer-24 projec-tions remain below τ24 on every turn: 0/143 crossings, with Wilson 95% upper bound 2.62% . A full-conversation assistant-token diagnostic on the subset with that readout available gives the same threshold result ( 0/110 crossings,

## 6. Discussion 

The three failures locate different breaks in the evidential chain from an interpretable readout to an agentic moni-tor. The disposition direction is a construction-valid pop-ulation contrast that does not transfer to in-context behav-ior. The prefill probe is a within-distribution predictive signal that weakens under domain transfer. The emotion-vector family is closest to the full mechanistic-precursor claim: its projections are semantically legible and its steer-ing interventions move action rates, but the predictive and semantic-specificity interpretations weaken under shared-prefix, bridge-probe, random-direction, learned-control, and cross-model checks. These distinctions matter because monitoring claims often combine several claims that need separate tests. A direction can separate the data used to build it without forecasting a fu-ture action. A hidden-state classifier can predict within one domain while mainly tracking prompt content. A steering direction can be causal without being concept-specific. The practical evaluation question is therefore not only whether an internal readout looks meaningful, but which claim it is being asked to support: construction validity, pre-action prediction, or a specific mechanistic interpretation. 

## 7. Limitations 

We do not show that pre-action internal monitoring is impos-sible. We test three natural probe families, specific models, specific layers or layer bands, and a finite set of scenar-ios. Each family is evaluated on its primary model; only the emotion-vector family has a second-model (Qwen3-32B) replication, so whether the disposition-direction or prefill-probe negative results recur on other model fami-lies is untested. Sparse-autoencoder features (Templeton et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024), sequence-level probes, larger nonlinear classifiers, and probes trained directly across many tool-action scenarios remain open; our evi-dence therefore bears more directly on lightweight probe formulations (linear and shallow-MLP readouts) than on richer representation-learning or temporal-aggregation mon-itors. The emotion-vector results are not a faithful replication of Sofroniew et al. (2026). We use open-weight models, a smaller emotion vocabulary, a single global steering norm, and learned semantic controls not present in the original study. These divergences make our claims narrower but also define our methodological contribution: the added controls test semantic specificity more directly than random vectors alone. The blackmail outcome is judge-operationalized. A small human spot-check found imperfect but usable agreement (0.8), so the absolute rates should be read as rubric-conditional. Our main claims use within-experiment con-trasts and control comparisons rather than treating the judge as ground truth. The judge also enters only the emotion-vector experiments: the disposition-direction threshold re-sult and the shared-prefix minimal-pair result are judge-independent geometric measurements, and the prefill probe uses continue-vs-correct labels from a separate labeling model rather than the alignment judge. Non-rejections such as the calm-versus-random comparison are not equivalence 8Internal-State Probes Read the Situation, Not the Action 

tests. The geography -near-random comparison is descrip-tive only. The steering experiments also use one main black-mail scenario, and the four learned content controls are a small sample of possible unrelated concepts. Finally, the negative results are scoped by clustering, sam-pling, and measurement choices. The disposition-direction evaluation covers 11 misaligned Petri-style scenarios and 1,986 Qwen prefill rows, but the audit contexts are clus-tered by transcript; the Qwen prefill corpus is text-only, partially overlaps the construction prompts, and measures a different token position. The full-conversation disposition readout is useful as a during-turn diagnostic, but it is not the primary pre-action test. The prefill domains have uneven class skews, the shared-prefix minimal pair rules out only pre-difference prediction rather than post-cue pre-action pre-diction, and the Gemma bridge probe has one near-threshold within-condition AUC ( 0.640 ). We therefore treat the re-sults as evidence against strong monitor claims under these protocols, not as tight estimates of a universal null. 

## 8. Conclusion 

We tested whether internal-state readouts that succeed in their construction settings can support pre-action monitoring claims. Across a fine-tune/base direction, behavioral prefill probes, and emotion-concept vectors, the tested readouts do not become robust monitors for future misaligned text or tool actions under our controls. This is not a claim that no such pre-action signal exists. Concatenating residual, attention-output, and MLP-output streams across L24, L31, L41, L51 (21,504-dim per row) and reducing with PCA at 100 or 200 components, fitted per training fold, does not exceed the single-layer bound. The best of 12 multi-layer configurations (3 streams × 2 PCA budgets × 2 probe kinds: logistic regression and MLP) reaches AUC 0.610 ± 0.033 on the attention-output PCA-100 logistic regression — below the single-layer best of 0.632 on attention-output at L24. Leave-one-condition-out mean lift on the same best probe is −25 .8 pp (4/4 splits ≤ 0). MLP probes under-perform logistic regression on the same features in 6/6 matched stream ×PCA pairings (best MLP AUC 0.574 ). A 200-permutation label-shuffle null on the best configuration places the trained probe at z = 3 .25 

above null mean ( 0.502 ± 0.033 ), but this null is conditional on the already-selected best configuration and does not absorb best-of-12 selection. 

Within-condition probe. A per-condition probe asks whether two rollouts that share the scenario, user prompt, steering condition, and prefill fraction differ pre-action. We trained 10 residual-only configurations per condition (4 single-layer 

× 2 probe kinds plus 1 multi-layer concat × 2 probe kinds; n ≈ 297 per condition) on the bridging-probe prefill features. Per-condition best AUCs are 0.593 (baseline), 0.558 (calm , α = 0 .05 ), 0.575 (desperate , α = 0 .10 ), and 0.640 (random ,

α = 0 .10 ); pooled mean 0.591 . All three pre-specified gates (per-condition AUC < 0.65 , pooled mean < 0.65 , no condition AUC > 0.75 ) pass. Caveat: the random -direction condition’s best AUC of 0.640 is only 0.010 below the gate threshold. This is a condition-controlled prediction check, not an identical-prefix minimal-pair test: the prefill states include rollout-specific truncated assistant prefixes, so the per-row hidden state is not byte-identical across rollouts within a condition. 

Four semantic-control directions. We evaluate four content-coded directions, cats , weather , sports , and geography ,extracted by the same difference-of-means plus neutral-PCA pipeline and applied at L41 of Gemma-3-27B-IT under the throughout-steering plugin at the same global steering norm. Sample size is n = 300 per condition, and the canonical multi-dimensional alignment judge is reused. Storygen corpus sizes are 493 sports stories, 485 geography stories, 491 

weather stories, and 200 cats stories. The smaller cats corpus is an inherited asymmetry from the cats-control run, so diff-of-means estimation is approximately equal-power for sports / geography / weather and noisier for cats .
